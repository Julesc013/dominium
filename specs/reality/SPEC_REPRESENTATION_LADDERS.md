# Dominium Representation Ladders v1

## A. Purpose And Scope

This document defines the constitutional framework for representation ladders in Dominium after `Λ-0`, `Λ-1`, and `Λ-2`.

Representation ladders are needed because the same lawful reality may appear in:

- primitive truth-bearing forms
- recognized structures
- formalized structures
- substituted or capsuled forms
- local detailed forms
- large-scale summarized forms
- observer-dependent projections that still preserve the same substrate meaning

Without an explicit ladder framework, later systems would be forced to improvise transitions between detail levels, semantic levels, and summary forms.
That would invite silent semantic drift, fake ontology, and runtime convenience masquerading as lawful world modeling.

This prompt defines:

- what a representation ladder is
- the framework-level ladder families
- what variation between ladder levels is lawful
- what invariant categories later domains must preserve explicitly
- what makes substitution, collapse, and expansion legitimate
- how macro and micro coexist without contradiction
- what later domains and later runtime work must not do

This prompt does not yet define:

- every domain's concrete ladder instances
- every proof or equivalence method for substitution
- runtime materialization services
- streaming, caching, or graphics systems
- ownership winners in quarantine-sensitive semantic zones

This framework inherits from `Λ-0` and `Λ-1`.
It is also informed by existing repo embodiment in `docs/architecture/macro_capsules.md`, `docs/worldgen/REFINEMENT_CONTRACT.md`, `docs/worldgen/REFINEMENT_LATTICE.md`, `docs/worldgen/UNIVERSE_SCALE_STRATEGY.md`, `docs/system/MACROCAPSULE_BEHAVIOR_MODEL.md`, `docs/specs/SPEC_LOD.md`, and live world/domain baseline material.

## B. Core Definition

A representation ladder is a lawful family of representations of the same underlying reality, organized so movement between them can occur without semantic drift.

The ladder idea exists because:

- truth is total
- materialization is sparse
- different horizons require different levels of detail or abstraction
- the same world must support embodied, abstract, institutional, and large-scale use without becoming multiple incompatible worlds

Representation ladders are broader than graphical LOD.

They cover:

- semantic structure
- formal structure
- substitution and capsule structure
- macro and micro scale structure
- continuity and equivalence across lawful representations

They do not mean:

- renderer-only detail bands
- texture or mesh quality swaps
- camera-distance shortcuts that invent or erase meaning

Graphical LOD may sit on top of representation ladders, but it is not the ladder framework itself.

Resolved ambiguity:

- representation ladder change is not merely graphical LOD

## C. Ladder Families

At the constitutional level, Dominium recognizes the following ladder families.

These families are framework-level and reusable across domains.
They are not necessarily one single linear staircase.
Domains may use subsets, intersections, or nested combinations of them later.

### C.1 Primitive Representation

Primitive representation is the lowest-level truth-bearing representation a domain treats as authoritative for its scope.

It is where:

- direct substrate commitments live
- primitive identity anchors live
- conserved or truth-bearing units are declared
- Process mutation ultimately grounds itself

Primitive representation is not automatically "smallest visual detail."
It is the domain's lowest declared truth-bearing layer.

### C.2 Recognized Representation

Recognized representation is a representation in which meaningful patterns or structures have been recognized over primitive substrate.

Recognized representation may identify:

- roads
- routes
- machines
- settlements
- institutions
- ecological communities
- logistics networks

Recognition does not replace substrate.
It expresses structured meaning over substrate that remains traceable to what the domain actually owns.

### C.3 Formalized Representation

Formalized representation is a representation in which recognized meaning has been stabilized into explicit formal structures, contracts, standards, institutions, blueprints, or declared system roles.

This can include:

- standard component families
- institutional roles
- legal structures
- industrial categories
- blueprint-backed components
- formal route or governance artifacts

Formalized representation is not mere naming.
It is explicit, reusable, and constrained by declared rules.

### C.4 Substituted Or Capsuled Representation

Substituted or capsuled representation is a lawful stand-in representation used in place of a more detailed or differently structured representation for a declared horizon.

It may be:

- a macro capsule
- a summary state
- a condensed proxy
- a bounded equivalent representation

This family exists to support sparse materialization and bounded computation without treating optimization as permission to fake ontology.

### C.5 Macro Summary Representation

Macro summary representation is a large-scale, condensed representation that preserves the invariants required for broader-scale reasoning and later lawful expansion.

This can include:

- summarized weather
- population or trust summaries
- institution graphs
- resource distributions
- system-scale transport or ecology summaries
- civilization-scale historical summaries

Macro summary is lawful only when it preserves the commitments needed for its current horizon and future lawful descent.

### C.6 Micro Detailed Representation

Micro detailed representation is a locally expanded or fine-grained representation used when the current horizon requires more detail.

This can include:

- local region detail
- embodied interaction detail
- detailed machine state
- fine-grained ecological or infrastructural structure
- detailed Process-relevant state

Micro detail is not intrinsically more true than macro summary.
It is a different lawful resolution of the same underlying truth commitments for a narrower or more demanding horizon.

## D. Lawful Variation Versus Semantic Drift

Representations may vary lawfully in:

- fidelity
- granularity
- abstraction
- serialization
- storage strategy
- access scope
- projection shape
- computational cost

They must preserve the declared meaning they claim to preserve.

Representation change is lawful only when all of the following hold:

- preserved invariants are explicit
- causal horizon is explicit
- activation and refusal conditions are explicit
- continuity obligations remain intact
- the representation does not silently gain or lose meaning outside its declared scope

Representation change is not permission to:

- rewrite ontology
- erase provenance
- alter identity
- invent new capabilities
- silently coerce compatibility or legal state
- change historical commitments without declaration

Optimization may change representation.
Optimization may not change meaning silently.

Resolved ambiguity:

- substitution may not silently change meaning

## E. Invariants And Preservation

Every ladder or representation family must declare the invariant categories it preserves.

At the framework level, the main invariant categories are:

- `identity_and_provenance`:
  stable identities, origins, and deterministic causal lineage
- `topology_and_connectivity`:
  adjacency, route connectivity, structural attachment, or network relations that must not silently break
- `conservation_relevant_quantities`:
  mass, stock, flow, energy, counts, or other domain-relevant conserved or bounded quantities
- `behavioral_equivalence_classes`:
  lawful outcome classes that must remain equivalent for the active horizon
- `capability_and_access_relationships`:
  lawful action relationships, permissions, and constraints that remain true across the representation change
- `compatibility_relationships`:
  explicit compatibility or incompatibility commitments
- `historical_lineage`:
  event lineage, state lineage, and persistence of prior commitments
- `institutional_continuity`:
  continuity of organizations, legal structures, standards, or governance identity where applicable
- `epistemic_traceability`:
  ability to explain what is known, summarized, latent, or uncertain

Later domains must refine which of these matter and what additional invariants they require.
No domain may treat preserved invariants as optional if it uses ladder movement.

## F. Legitimacy Of Substitution

Substitution is lawful only when it has an explicit legitimacy basis.

That basis must include:

- a declared source and target representation
- preserved invariants
- a declared causal horizon for validity
- explicit activation conditions
- explicit expansion or escalation triggers
- explicit refusal conditions
- enough lineage and provenance to keep the substitution auditable

Substitution is not lawful if it is merely convenient.

The following cannot be substituted implicitly:

- identity anchors
- legal or institutional identity
- historical commitments that remain relevant
- route or topology commitments that still affect outcomes
- capability commitments required for current interaction
- compatibility commitments that downstream systems rely on

Whether representation change should be visible, invisible, or prohibited depends on the current lawful horizon:

- `invisible` when invariant-preserving movement produces no contradiction for the active actor and causal horizon
- `visible` when law, epistemics, or interface obligations require the change to be surfaced explicitly
- `prohibited` when preserved invariants, continuity, or causal closure cannot be maintained

## G. Macro To Micro Transitions

Macro and micro are not rival truths.
They are lawful granularities over the same substrate.

Macro and micro may coexist when:

- one horizon needs broad-scale summary
- another horizon needs local detailed resolution
- both remain bound to the same preserved commitments

When expanding macro back into micro, the system must preserve:

- identity and provenance anchors already committed
- topology and adjacency already committed
- conservation-relevant quantities already committed
- capabilities and constraints already committed
- historical and institutional lineage already committed

Expansion must not invent detail freely.
It must either:

- recover detail from authoritative substrate
- derive lawful detail from preserved commitments and declared generative rules
- preserve explicit uncertainty where unique recovery is not possible
- or refuse when lawful expansion is underdetermined

When collapsing micro into macro, the system must preserve:

- enough summary state for later lawful expansion
- enough invariant state for later interactions to remain correct
- enough lineage to keep later explanation and audit valid
- enough commitment to prevent later semantic drift

Later civilization, infrastructure, ecology, logistics, and world systems depend on this because they must move between local detail and large-scale summary without turning the world into disconnected simulations.

## H. Continuity Doctrine For Ladder Movement

Experiential continuity applies to ladder movement.

Across representation change, continuity means:

- the same object, place, institution, route, or social structure remains intelligibly the same unless lawful Process changed it
- prior lawful observation is not retroactively contradicted without cause
- scale changes do not create arbitrary teleports, impossible disappearances, or unexplained replacements
- higher-order summary remains consistent with what later detailed re-entry reveals

Unacceptable discontinuity includes:

- a road network summarized as connected that later expands into disconnected fragments without lawful cause
- a recognized institution collapsing into a generic summary that erases its continuity even though its identity still matters
- a macro summary implying safety, availability, or capacity that micro expansion contradicts without lawful intervening Process
- a civilization capsule expanding into arbitrary details with no lineage constraints

Continuity does not require visual sameness.
It requires lawful semantic sameness for the active horizon.

## I. Domain Ownership Of Ladders

Each domain must define its concrete ladder instances later.

That includes:

- which representations it recognizes
- what invariants each preserves
- how it permits substitution, collapse, or expansion
- what refusal conditions apply
- what scale bands it uses

No domain may define ladder behavior that contradicts this framework.

Cross-domain ladders must be handled explicitly later.
They must not be hidden inside one domain's convenience layer or runtime shortcut.

This matters especially for:

- civilization and institution summaries
- logistics and infrastructure overlays
- ecological and agricultural systems
- multi-domain materialization boundaries

## J. Anti-Patterns And Forbidden Ladder Shapes

The following are forbidden:

- graphics-only LOD treated as full semantic substitution
- collapsing rich truth into summary without declared preserved invariants
- expanding summary into detail that contradicts preserved history or identity
- substituting representations that erase legal, cultural, industrial, or institutional identity that still matters
- runtime convenience redefining ladder semantics by implementation accident
- treating camera distance alone as sufficient ladder authority
- using capsules to hide unresolved domain ownership
- treating macro summary as permission to drop provenance or auditability

## K. Stability And Evolution

Stability class: `stable`.

This artifact is stable enough for later `Λ`, `Σ`, and `Φ` prompts to cite directly.

Later prompts may:

- define domain-specific ladder instances
- refine equivalence and proof criteria
- specialize invariants per domain
- add explicit bridge rules across domains

Later prompts may not:

- silently override the difference between semantic level and graphics detail
- redefine substitution as arbitrary replacement
- allow ladder movement to erase provenance, continuity, or identity
- reduce macro or capsule reasoning to renderer-driven convenience

Any change to the meaning of ladder families, lawful substitution, or continuity obligations requires:

- explicit artifact update
- explicit versioning
- explicit downstream semantic impact review
