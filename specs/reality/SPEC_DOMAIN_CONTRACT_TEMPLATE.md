# Domain Contract Template Standard v1

## A. Purpose And Scope

This document defines the standard constitutional shape of a lawful Domain contract in Dominium.

A Domain contract exists to prevent domains from entering the project as:

- loose features
- isolated subsystem lore
- implicit ontologies
- runtime-only convenience layers
- presentation-driven special cases

A lawful Domain is a coherent phenomenon or law-governed subject area that:

- owns explicit truth-bearing substrate within a declared scope
- defines lawful Process paths for authoritative change
- distinguishes primitive truth from recognized patterns and observed projections
- exposes explicit affordance, observation, verification, scaling, and extension rules
- inherits the universal reality framework instead of redefining it locally

Domains may be physical, biological, ecological, social, institutional, informational, infrastructural, economic, legal, epistemic, diegetic, or cross-cutting.

This template governs the standard form every domain constitution must follow.
It does not yet define all domain instances, all capability surfaces, all representation ladders, or all cross-domain bridges.

## B. Relationship To Higher Doctrine

Every domain contract is subordinate to:

1. `docs/canon/constitution_v1.md`
2. `docs/canon/glossary_v1.md`
3. `AGENTS.md`
4. `specs/reality/SPEC_DOMINIUM_UNIVERSAL_REALITY_FRAMEWORK.md`

This template inherits the following from the Constitutional Architecture and `Λ-0`:

- Truth / Perceived / Render separation
- determinism and Process-only mutation
- law-gated authority and refusal
- pack-driven integration
- truth totality
- sparse materialization
- minimal causal closure
- lawful representation and substitution
- experiential continuity
- macro/micro consistency
- interface-agnostic reality

This template also prepares later work:

- `Λ-2` builds capability surfaces over domain affordances
- `Λ-3` refines the representation and substitution sections
- `Λ-4` refines recognitions, formalizations, and substitution proofs
- `Λ-6` builds bridges between instantiated domains without erasing ownership boundaries
- `Σ` later governs work against this structure
- `Φ` later maps runtime and service boundaries onto lawful domain structure using this shape

This template does not resolve the live `schema` versus `schemas`, `packs` versus `data/packs`, or `field` versus `fields` splits.
Instead, it requires domain instances to expose overlap and ownership sensitivity explicitly.

Resolved ambiguity:

- a domain may not define its own local reality doctrine
- `schema/**` contract law may not be ignored
- `schemas/**` may not be treated as automatic replacement authority
- mirrored pack or field surfaces may not be silently normalized by template convenience

## C. Domain Identity And Scope

Every domain contract MUST define explicit identity and explicit scope.

Identity MUST include:

- a stable `domain_id`
- a domain name and summary
- the phenomenon classes the domain claims to govern
- the truth classes it claims to own directly
- the repo roots or artifact families it is grounded in

Scope MUST include:

- what the domain governs
- what it explicitly does not govern
- the boundary claims it makes
- known overlap zones or ownership sensitivities
- notes about how overlap is contained without silent takeover

Domain scope MUST NOT be implied from directory names alone.
Domain scope MUST NOT be "everything related to X."
If a domain has uncertain overlap with another domain or with a quarantined repo surface, that overlap MUST be declared explicitly rather than hand-waved away.

## D. Primitive Substrate

Every domain contract MUST define its primitive substrate.

The primitive substrate is the lowest-level truth-bearing substrate the domain treats as authoritative for its scope.
Primitives may include:

- state units
- entities
- fields
- relations
- commitments
- conserved quantities
- boundary conditions

The contract MUST state:

- what counts as primitive
- what invariants primitives preserve
- what truth classes primitives directly carry
- what repo or artifact roots ground those primitives

Higher-order meaning MUST NOT be smuggled into primitives without declaration.
If a concept is already recognized, interpreted, institutional, or interface-facing, it is not primitive merely because the contract writer wants a shorter document.

## E. Processes

Every domain contract MUST define lawful Processes for authoritative transformation within the domain.

The contract MUST state:

- which Processes may mutate truth
- what their inputs are
- what their outputs are
- what invariants they preserve
- what refusal or degradation conditions apply

Authoritative mutation MUST happen through Processes, not side effects, not UI events, not renderer code, and not ad hoc scripts.

If the domain has no internal mutation path of its own, the contract MUST say so explicitly and explain which external Process families lawfully affect it.
Silence is not an acceptable Process model.

## F. Recognitions

Every domain contract MUST define its recognition layer.

Recognitions are structured patterns, emergent classifications, or derived meanings recognized over primitive truth and Process history.

The contract MUST state:

- what recognitions exist or that none are currently declared
- what primitives or Process outputs recognitions derive from
- what validity conditions apply
- whether recognition is observer-independent or epistemically filtered

Recognitions MUST remain distinct from primitive truth.
Recognitions may summarize, classify, or signal meaning.
They may not replace primitives as the source of authoritative truth.

Resolved ambiguity:

- recognitions may not stand in for primitive substrate
- recognitions may not be used to hide missing primitive definitions

## G. Affordances

Every domain contract MUST define its affordance layer.

Affordances are the actionable capability relationships the domain exposes.
They describe what kinds of actors, systems, or Assemblies may attempt to do in relation to the domain under law, authority, and domain conditions.

The contract MUST state:

- which affordances exist or that none are currently declared
- who or what may invoke them
- which capabilities, authorities, or entitlements matter
- which Processes they route through
- what refusal or degradation conditions apply

Affordances are not free-form UX verbs.
They are law-gated action surfaces grounded in the domain's substrate and Process rules.

## H. Formalizations

Every domain contract MUST define its formalization posture.

Formalizations are explicit artifacts, standards, models, blueprints, protocols, institutions, or structured declarations the domain produces or depends on.

The contract MUST state:

- which formalizations are declared
- what they mean
- when they become applicable
- what normative references they bind to

If the domain does not yet define any formalizations, it MUST say `none declared` and explain why.

Formalizations are not required to be institutional or bureaucratic.
They are required to be explicit wherever the domain depends on standards, declarations, contracts, or institutionalized structure.

## I. Representation And Substitution

Every domain contract MUST declare its lawful representations.

The contract MUST state:

- which representations the domain uses
- what invariants each representation preserves
- what questions each representation can and cannot answer
- when expansion, escalation, or refusal is required

If the domain allows substitution, collapse, or capsule use, the contract MUST define:

- which substitutions are lawful
- which invariants are preserved
- what causal horizon they are valid for
- what continuity conditions apply
- what refusal conditions apply

If the domain allows no substitutions yet, the contract MUST state `none declared`.

Substitutions MUST NOT be implicit.
Capsules MUST NOT be hand-waved.
Any representation change that alters meaning silently is unlawful.

Resolved ambiguity:

- substitutions may not be implicit
- macro/micro collapse is lawful only when invariant preservation and causal closure are explicit

## J. Failure Modes

Every domain contract MUST define failure and degradation semantics.

The contract MUST distinguish:

- ordinary domain state
- degraded domain state
- invalid or unsafe state
- externally absent or unavailable state when relevant

The contract MUST state:

- failure triggers
- how failure affects truth
- how failure affects observation
- what refusal or degradation behavior follows
- what recovery, repair, or invalidation rules exist

Failure semantics MUST NOT be inferred from implementation behavior alone.
If failure meaning is not written down, the domain is under-specified.

## K. Verification

Every domain contract MUST define how the domain is verified.

The contract MUST state:

- which invariants later tooling should check
- what audit surfaces exist
- what replay or determinism expectations apply
- what proof or evidence artifacts should exist when the domain changes

Verification may include:

- schema checks
- static checks
- replay checks
- runtime invariants
- audit or inspection hooks
- manual review notes where full automation is not yet available

The absence of perfect automation does not remove the obligation to state what must be verified.

## L. Observation Bindings

Every domain contract MUST define how the domain becomes observable or inspectable.

The contract MUST state:

- which observation bindings exist
- which truth classes they derive from
- which epistemic artifacts or lenses they depend on
- what channels, summaries, or redactions they expose

Observation bindings MUST be derived from truth.
They MUST NOT be treated as truth structure themselves.
A domain may not use observation summaries as a substitute for missing truth-bearing substrate.

Resolved ambiguity:

- observation bindings may not stand in for truth structure
- observation may filter or summarize but may not become ontology

## M. Interface Bindings

Every domain contract MUST define how different interface classes interact with the domain.

At minimum, the contract MUST address:

- embodied users
- abstract planners
- AI agents
- NPC or institutional actors
- system-level automation

The contract MUST state for each class:

- whether interaction is supported, conditional, or not supported
- what interaction modes are lawful
- what observation dependencies exist
- what refusal conditions apply

This section does not globally define capability surfaces.
It records the domain-local interaction posture so later capability work can formalize it without guessing.

## N. Compatibility And Stability Metadata

Every domain contract MUST carry explicit compatibility and stability metadata.

At minimum, the contract MUST include:

- contract version
- stability class
- replacement target when provisional
- future series note when relevant
- compatibility considerations
- semantic contract pin back to `Λ-0`
- notes about ownership sensitivity or unresolved overlap

This metadata exists so semantic evolution is explicit.
It prevents domains from drifting by local reinterpretation or prompt convenience.

Compatibility and stability metadata MUST NOT be omitted just because a domain is early.
Early domains may be provisional.
They may not be unpinned.

## O. Civilization Scaling Path

Every domain contract MUST define its scaling path.

The contract MUST state:

- how the domain behaves across macro and micro scales
- what summary forms are lawful at larger scales
- what triggers more detailed realization
- what continuity requirements bind scale changes

Domains may have different scale shapes.
They do not all need a particle-like micro model.
They do all need an explicit account of how large-scale summaries, local detail, and continuity coexist.

Domains that interact with civilization, institutions, logistics, infrastructure, ecology, or settlement systems MUST be especially explicit here because later cross-domain work depends on lawful scale transitions.

## P. Mod And Extension Path

Every domain contract MUST define how packs, mods, and future extensions may lawfully extend it.

The contract MUST state:

- declared pack or extension points
- what may be specialized
- what may not be overridden
- what compatibility rules apply
- what refusal rules apply when extensions are missing or invalid

Pack-driven integration remains binding.
Extensions may specialize lawful domain structure.
They may not replace core reality semantics, erase required invariants, or smuggle executable ontology into presentation or convenience layers.

If no extension points are currently exposed, the contract MUST say so explicitly.

## Q. Anti-Patterns And Forbidden Domain Shapes

The following domain shapes are unlawful:

- hidden ontology:
  the contract depends on unstated primitives, scope, or invariants
- local reality doctrine:
  the domain attempts to redefine Truth, materialization, causality, or continuity locally
- recognition-as-truth:
  recognized patterns are used instead of primitive truth
- presentation-driven semantics:
  UI, observation, or render projections define domain meaning
- ad hoc special casing:
  domain rules exist only as special behavior with no contract structure
- undefined ownership overlap:
  overlapping scope exists but is not declared
- unverified substitution:
  capsules or substitutions are implied without invariants and refusal conditions
- implicit failure semantics:
  failure is left to implementation accidents
- observation-as-truth inversion:
  observation bindings stand in for truth structure
- runtime-only convenience layer:
  the domain exists only as a service shape with no semantic contract

Later prompts should refuse domain constitutions that match these anti-patterns.

## R. Minimum Completeness Criteria

A domain contract is complete enough only if it defines, at minimum:

- explicit identity and scope
- primitive substrate
- Process model
- recognition model
- affordance model
- formalization posture
- representation and substitution posture
- failure model
- verification surfaces
- observation bindings
- interface bindings
- compatibility and stability metadata
- civilization scaling path
- mod and extension path

The following may remain provisional:

- detailed formalizations
- specific substitutions
- exact capability vocabulary
- exact bridge relationships to other domains
- detailed scale heuristics

The following may NOT remain implicit:

- inheritance from `Λ-0`
- scope and ownership sensitivity
- primitive substrate
- Process-only mutation rule
- recognition versus primitive distinction
- observation versus truth distinction
- substitution posture
- failure semantics
- verification floor
- extension/refusal posture

If any of those are missing, the domain contract is under-specified and later prompts should refuse to treat it as constitutionally valid.
