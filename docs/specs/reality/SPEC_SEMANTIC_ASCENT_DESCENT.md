# Dominium Semantic Ascent And Descent v1

## A. Purpose And Scope

This document defines the constitutional framework for semantic ascent and semantic descent in Dominium.

It exists because Dominium must support meaning that lawfully emerges across scale and structure, such as:

- paths becoming roads and corridors
- machine assemblies becoming recognized machines and later standardized component families
- settlement clusters becoming towns, cities, or industrial regions
- populations becoming ecological communities or agricultural regimes
- institutional fragments becoming formal governance, administrative, or legal systems

Without an explicit ascent and descent framework, later systems would be tempted to:

- treat labels as meaning
- treat higher-order semantics as arbitrary lore
- expand summaries into invented detail with no lineage constraints
- collapse institutions, standards, or civilizations into presentation shortcuts

This framework governs:

- what semantic ascent is
- what semantic descent is
- how they relate to representation ladders
- how they differ from recognition, formalization, and substitution
- how ambiguity and failure must be handled
- how higher-order meaning remains lawful across actors and scales

This framework does not yet define:

- every domain's ascent criteria
- every formalization chain
- every domain bridge
- runtime services for semantic materialization

It inherits directly from `Λ-0`, `Λ-1`, `Λ-2`, and `Λ-3`.

## B. Semantic Ascent

Semantic ascent is the lawful emergence of higher-order meaning from lower-order substrate, Process history, recognition structure, and preserved invariants.

Semantic ascent is not arbitrary naming.
It is not a presentation label.
It is not a UI category.
It is not a lore shortcut.

Semantic ascent requires an evidence basis grounded in domain structure.

At the framework level, ascent may look like:

- path -> road -> corridor
- machine assembly -> recognized machine -> blueprint-backed component family
- settlement cluster -> town -> industrial city
- species populations -> ecological community -> managed agriculture or biome regime
- institution fragments -> bureaucracy or governance system

The important rule is that ascent must be grounded in:

- lawful substrate
- Process history
- recognized structural patterns
- stable capability relationships
- preserved lineage
- domain-declared criteria

Ascent may therefore depend on:

- repeated structure
- stable Process roles
- recognized topological patterns
- formal or informal institutional uptake
- durable route or logistics importance
- preserved historical continuity

It must not depend only on:

- how something is rendered
- how a UI labels it
- camera distance
- an author's convenience
- a one-off descriptive tag with no substrate basis

Resolved ambiguity:

- semantic ascent is not arbitrary labeling

## C. Semantic Descent

Semantic descent is the lawful recovery, expansion, or projection of higher-order meaning back into lower-order detail that remains consistent with preserved invariants and lineage.

Descent exists because higher-order structures must sometimes be re-entered at lower scales or finer detail.

Examples include:

- a summarized road corridor being expanded into local route segments and nodes
- an industrial city summary being expanded into district, infrastructure, and institution detail
- a macro ecology summary being expanded into local habitat and population detail
- a civilization or governance summary being expanded into concrete institutions, constraints, and interactions

Semantic descent is not free invention.

Descent must remain bound by:

- preserved invariants
- prior commitments already made
- lineage and provenance
- domain rules
- lawful uncertainty when unique recovery is not possible

If the higher-order representation does not uniquely determine all lower-order detail, the system must:

- recover detail from authoritative substrate where available
- derive lawful detail from preserved commitments and declared generative rules
- preserve explicit uncertainty where detail remains underdetermined
- or refuse to overclaim

Resolved ambiguity:

- semantic descent may not invent details freely

## D. Recognition Versus Ascent

Recognition and ascent are related but not identical.

Recognition is a domain-level classification or identification event.
It says that some pattern, structure, or relation is present and meaningful.

Semantic ascent goes further.
It establishes a higher-order semantic level with its own lawful implications.

In practice:

- recognition may identify a settlement cluster
- ascent may lawfully stabilize that cluster as a town-scale semantic level
- recognition may identify repeated machine assemblies
- ascent may lawfully establish a reusable machine family or industrial role

Recognition can feed ascent.
Recognition is not the same thing as ascent.

Recognition says "this pattern exists."
Ascent says "this higher-order meaning lawfully emerges and is now usable at a higher semantic level."

## E. Formalization Versus Ascent

Formalization is not identical to ascent.

Semantic ascent may precede formalization.

For example:

- a corridor may exist before it is standardized
- a settlement may function like a city before it is formally classified as one
- an institution may behave as governance before explicit charter or legal formalization exists

Formalization stabilizes, declares, and propagates meaning for reuse.

It may produce:

- standards
- blueprints
- official institutions
- protocols
- recognized roles
- explicit contracts

Ascent can therefore produce meaning that later becomes formalized.
Formalization does not create every semantic level from nothing.
It codifies and stabilizes meaning that can already lawfully exist.

## F. Substitution Versus Ascent And Descent

Substitution is not the same thing as ascent or descent.

Substitution changes which lawful representation stands in for another for a declared horizon.
Ascent and descent describe semantic relation across levels of meaning.

A few distinctions matter:

- ascent does not automatically authorize substitution
- descent does not automatically require substitution
- substitution can occur within one ladder level or across multiple levels
- substitution is valid only when preserved invariants, continuity, and causal closure still hold

For example:

- a macro capsule may substitute for a more detailed regional state without creating a new semantic level
- a city summary may represent an ascended semantic level, but descent into district detail is not itself the same operation as substitution

These concepts must stay separate so later prompts can define proof, refusal, and runtime behavior clearly.

## G. Multi-Actor And Institutional Significance

Semantic ascent and descent must support more than one actor type.

They must remain meaningful for:

- players
- NPCs
- AI agents
- institutions
- civilizations
- system-level automation and planning

Higher-order meaning may become:

- socially significant
- legally significant
- industrially significant
- politically significant
- historically significant

That means ascent and descent cannot be treated as mere rendering conveniences.

Examples:

- a road corridor may matter for logistics, law, infrastructure planning, and player travel
- an institution may matter for legal authority, refusal conditions, capability exposure, and historical continuity
- a civilization summary may matter for trust, trade, law, conflict, education, and knowledge systems

This is why ascent and descent must remain grounded in substrate and lineage rather than presentation.

## H. Failure And Ambiguity

Semantic ascent may fail, remain provisional, or remain contested.

Examples of lawful ambiguity include:

- a settlement cluster that does not yet satisfy the domain's city-level criteria
- a machine pattern that is recognized but not stable enough to count as a standardized family
- an institutional fragment that does not yet lawfully constitute a governance system
- a civilization or history summary that remains contested or uncertainty-bound

Semantic descent may also fail or be unsafe when:

- preserved invariants are insufficient
- lineage is incomplete
- the current representation underdetermines too much detail
- expansion would overclaim beyond lawful evidence

When ambiguity or failure occurs, the system must prefer:

- provisional classification
- explicit uncertainty
- explicit refusal
- explicit escalation

It must not silently guess.

## I. Anti-Patterns And Forbidden Shapes

The following are forbidden:

- declaring something "a city," "a factory," "a road," or "a governance system" solely because of presentation
- expanding a civilization capsule into arbitrary details with no lineage constraints
- using ascent as a hidden shortcut for content invention
- using descent as a free lore generator detached from truth
- treating recognition, ascent, formalization, and substitution as the same operation
- using runtime convenience or camera distance as the authority for semantic level changes
- erasing historical, legal, or institutional continuity in the course of ascent or descent

## J. Stability And Evolution

Stability class: `stable`.

This artifact is stable enough for later `Λ`, `Σ`, and `Φ` prompts to cite directly.

Later prompts may:

- define domain-specific ascent criteria
- define formalization chains
- define cross-domain bridge semantics
- define more detailed proof and refusal criteria

Later prompts may not:

- reduce ascent to arbitrary labeling
- reduce descent to unconstrained invention
- silently collapse ascent, recognition, formalization, and substitution into one concept
- override the continuity and lineage obligations frozen here

Any future change to the meaning of ascent, descent, or their relation to ladders requires:

- explicit artifact update
- explicit versioning
- explicit downstream semantic impact review
