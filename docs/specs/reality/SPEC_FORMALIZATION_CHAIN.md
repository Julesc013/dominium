# Dominium Formalization Chain v1

## A. Purpose And Scope

This document defines the constitutional formalization chain for Dominium after `Λ-0` through `Λ-3`.

It exists because Dominium must support more than local novelty or one-off discovery.
The project needs one lawful way to explain how useful novelty, practice, and structure become:

- recognized knowledge
- explicit capture
- formal specification
- validated method or artifact
- reusable standard
- institutionally embedded form
- scaled production, diffusion, or civilizational deployment
- revised, deprecated, superseded, or retired lineage

This framework governs:

- the meaning of formalization in Dominium
- the canonical stages of the formalization chain
- what transitions between stages require
- how lineage, validation, adoption, and supersession remain explicit
- how later domains, governance systems, runtime systems, and provenance systems must reason about reusable designs, methods, standards, and institutions

This framework does not yet define:

- every domain-specific formalization chain
- concrete runtime blueprint or manufacturing systems
- every institution or standards body
- all later provenance/versioning mechanics reserved for `Υ`
- ownership winners in quarantine-sensitive semantic zones reserved for later review

This framework inherits from `Λ-0` through `Λ-3`.
It does not create a parallel ontology.
It formalizes repo-grounded direction already visible in surfaces such as `materials/blueprint_engine.py`, `materials/maintenance/decay_engine.py`, `infrastructure/formalization/inference_engine.py`, `docs/worldgen/CIVILIZATIONS_AND_HISTORY_BASELINE.md`, `docs/worldgen/LAW_AND_GOVERNANCE_BASELINE.md`, `docs/knowledge/EPISTEMICS_OVERVIEW.md`, and existing planning artifacts that already treat standards, blueprints, protocols, institutions, and diffusion as first-class future concerns.

## B. Core Definition

Formalization in Dominium is the lawful process by which knowledge, design, practice, or structure becomes explicit, portable, reviewable, reusable, and lineage-bearing beyond its immediate local context.

Formalization is distinct from:

- `recognition`:
  recognition says a meaningful pattern or structure exists
- `semantic_ascent`:
  ascent says a higher-order meaning lawfully emerges
- `substitution`:
  substitution says one representation may stand in for another for a declared horizon
- `crafting_recipe_systems`:
  recipes are only one possible operational expression of a much broader formalization space
- `mere_documentation`:
  notes alone do not create a stable, verified, reusable constitutional artifact

Formalization is needed as a distinct layer because Dominium must support:

- reusable machine and part families
- explicit process pipelines
- inspection and measurement methods
- operating practices
- protocols and interface signatures
- standards and certifications
- laws, procedures, and institutions
- educational and industrial propagation
- revision and supersession without silent provenance loss

Resolved ambiguity:

- recognition alone does not create reusable standards
- blueprints are not just crafting recipes
- formalization does not automatically imply standardization
- standards do not automatically imply universal adoption

## C. Chain Stages

The constitutional formalization chain contains the following general stages.
Domains may refine them later, but they may not silently collapse or reorder their semantics.

1. `discovery`
2. `recognition`
3. `explicit_capture`
4. `specification_blueprinting`
5. `validation_testing`
6. `standardization`
7. `institutional_adoption`
8. `production_deployment_diffusion`
9. `maintenance_revision`
10. `deprecation_supersession_retirement`

These are not automatic transitions.
Passing one stage does not silently grant the next.
Each stage has its own inputs, evidence requirements, actor requirements, and refusal conditions.

## D. Discovery

Discovery is the stage at which something new, useful, or lawfully relevant becomes present to actors or systems.

Discovery may include:

- accidental findings
- experimental outcomes
- local process improvements
- novel assemblies or part arrangements
- newly measured regularities
- newly recognized institutional practices
- emergent route, logistics, or social structures

Discovery is not yet portable knowledge by default.
It may be:

- private
- local
- partial
- uncertain
- untrusted
- difficult to reproduce

Local or private discovery may remain valid within its local context without yet becoming public or reusable.
What it does not become automatically is civilization-grade knowledge.

## E. Recognition

Recognition is the stage in which a discovery or emergent pattern is identified as meaningful.

Recognition may determine that:

- a pattern is real
- a machine assembly is repeatable enough to matter
- a process produces stable results
- a route or institution has recognizable function
- a measurement seems diagnostically useful

Recognition may precede formalization and often should.
Recognition alone does not create a portable standard, blueprint, or institutionally trusted artifact.

Recognition answers:

- "this matters"
- "this pattern exists"
- "this may be reusable"

It does not yet answer:

- "this is fully specified"
- "this is validated"
- "this is standardized"
- "this is institutionally adopted"

## F. Explicit Capture

Useful knowledge, design, process, or practice must be captured explicitly before it becomes stable and portable.

Explicit capture is the stage where information is made durable enough for later review, transfer, refinement, or formal specification.

Capture classes may include:

- design sketch
- process description
- measurement protocol
- doctrine or policy statement
- interface signature
- workflow or operating practice
- material handling notes
- institutional charter fragment
- educational or procedural notes

Explicit capture is broader than writing notes.
It requires enough structure that later actors can inspect what was meant, what was observed, and what remains uncertain.

If useful knowledge is not explicitly captured, it remains fragile, local, and difficult to propagate without semantic drift.

## G. Specification And Blueprinting

Specification or blueprinting is the stage where explicit capture becomes a stable declared form with defined scope, structure, and intended reuse.

A specification or blueprint may describe:

- a component family
- an assembly layout
- an operating sequence
- an inspection method
- a protocol
- an institutional procedure
- a legal or administrative form
- an educational program

The distinction between informal notes and formal specification matters.

Informal notes:

- record useful material
- may remain provisional
- may lack completeness or stable boundaries

Formal specification or blueprinting:

- declares structure explicitly
- defines what is in scope
- declares constraints and expectations
- supports later verification, revision, and reuse

Blueprints and specifications are constitutional artifacts, not mere UX objects.
They are lineage-bearing forms that later systems may govern, validate, route, propagate, or refuse.

## H. Validation And Testing

Formalized artifacts may require validation before broader trust or deployment.

Validation is the stage where a captured or specified form is checked against relevant criteria.
Validation is not identical to standardization.

Validation classes may include:

- functional validation
- dimensional validation
- behavioral validation
- compliance validation
- safety or reliability validation
- social or institutional validation
- reproducibility validation
- measurement-method validation
- protocol interoperability validation

Validation may be:

- local
- organizational
- public
- provisional
- contested
- domain-specific

Validation is not assumed universal or perfect.
It is still required to be explicit when broader trust depends on it.

## I. Standardization

Standardization is the stage where a formalized and sufficiently validated artifact, method, or practice becomes a declared reusable coordination form.

Standards may be:

- local private standards
- organizational standards
- public or common standards
- interoperable protocol standards
- educational or inspection standards
- legal or administrative standards

A standard is not just "something written down."
A standard is a reusable coordination device.
It stabilizes expectations across repeated use, training, coordination, procurement, inspection, or governance.

Standardization does not automatically imply:

- universal truth for all domains
- universal adoption
- irreversibility
- absence of competing standards

It means a formalized artifact has crossed into explicit reusable coordination.

## J. Institutional Adoption

Institutional adoption is the stage where standards, specifications, or methods become embedded in organizations, traditions, legal structures, or social systems.

Institutional adoption may involve:

- guilds
- schools
- factories
- bureaucracies
- engineering traditions
- legal systems
- military doctrine
- scientific communities
- logistical networks
- religious or civic institutions where applicable

Institutional adoption is a major semantic shift.
It changes the role of a formalized artifact from "defined and reusable" to "embedded in repeatable social, administrative, or industrial structure."

Institutional adoption is not a minor metadata toggle.
It changes who teaches, enforces, certifies, procures, repeats, and depends on the artifact or method.

## K. Production, Deployment, And Diffusion

Production, deployment, and diffusion are the stages where a formalized artifact, process, or institution spreads into active use.

The framework distinguishes:

- one-off use
- repeated manufacture or repeated performance
- scaled deployment
- networked or civilizational diffusion

Diffusion may depend on:

- logistics
- labor
- education
- infrastructure
- institutional backing
- access to materials, tools, or measurement methods
- protocol compatibility
- trust and certification

Diffusion is not automatic once a standard exists.
A standard may remain local, underused, contested, or blocked by material or institutional conditions.

## L. Maintenance And Revision Lineage

Formalized artifacts must support maintenance and revision.

Maintenance and revision require explicit lineage for:

- what changed
- why it changed
- what remains compatible
- what is superseded
- what provenance chain connects earlier and later forms

Revision must not silently erase historical identity.

The chain must preserve:

- artifact identity
- provenance
- validation status
- compatibility posture
- institutional commitments where they still matter

Revision may:

- refine
- correct
- extend
- narrow
- fork
- reconcile

It may not silently rewrite history.

Resolved ambiguity:

- revision may not silently erase provenance

## M. Deprecation, Supersession, And Retirement

Formalized artifacts do not merely disappear.
They transition through explicit status states.

The framework distinguishes:

- `deprecated_but_supported`
- `obsolete`
- `superseded`
- `forbidden`
- `archival_historical`

These are not interchangeable.

For example:

- deprecated but supported forms remain lawful but discouraged
- obsolete forms may no longer be appropriate but still matter historically
- superseded forms have an explicit successor lineage
- forbidden forms are prohibited under current law or institutional policy
- archival forms remain part of history, provenance, or education even when no longer active

Later `Υ` work will refine versioning, naming, and release/provenance mechanisms.
It must extend this semantic lineage floor rather than override it.

## N. Formalization Targets

Formalization may apply to more than physical manufacture.

The constitutional target classes include:

- physical artifacts
- machine assemblies
- process pipelines
- interface signatures
- protocols
- standards
- institutions
- educational programs
- laws and governance procedures
- measurement and inspection methods
- scientific theories or models where a domain declares them relevant
- logistical and administrative procedures

This breadth is required so Dominium can model believable industry, science, bureaucracy, law, and civilization without reducing everything to item recipes.

## O. Formalization Actors

Multiple actor classes may participate in the chain.

The framework supports:

- players
- NPCs
- AI agents
- institutions
- organizations
- civilizations
- toolchains or automated systems acting within explicit authority limits

Participation is stage-sensitive.
Different actors may:

- discover
- capture
- validate
- standardize
- adopt
- deploy
- revise
- deprecate

within different law, authority, capability, and epistemic constraints.

No actor class automatically owns the whole chain.

## P. Preconditions And Constraints

Formalization stages may be gated by:

- access
- knowledge
- authority
- infrastructure
- materials
- measurement capability
- tool or machine availability
- institutional support
- protocol compatibility
- education or skill
- documentation quality
- verification cost

Formalization is not assumed universally available.
It remains compatible with epistemic limits, capability surfaces, law-gated authority, and sparse materialization.

If a stage lacks required preconditions, the system should:

- refuse advancement
- retain provisional status
- preserve local-only use
- or escalate for explicit review

It should not silently promote a local practice into a reusable standard.

## Q. Relationship To Representation Ladders

Formalization is related to, but distinct from, ladder movement and semantic ascent.

A thing may ascend semantically without yet being formalized.
For example:

- a corridor may exist before a route specification
- a city may exist before formal zoning or industrial standards
- an institution may function before charter or codified doctrine

Formalization may later stabilize, constrain, or propagate that meaning.

Formalization therefore:

- can follow recognition
- can follow ascent
- can stabilize later ladder movement
- can shape which substituted or macro forms are lawful later

What it does not do is replace ascent or ladders.
It remains a separate lineage layer concerned with explicit reusable form.

## R. Relationship To Substitution And Capsules

Formalization may enable lawful substitution later, but it does not automatically authorize substitution.

For example:

- a standard may define when substitution is permitted
- a blueprint may define what counts as compatible component replacement
- an institutional procedure may define acceptable proxies or delegated forms

But formalization alone is not enough.
Substitution still requires the preserved-invariant and causal-closure rules frozen in `Λ-0` and `Λ-3`.

This distinction prevents:

- specification from becoming fake ontology
- standards from becoming blanket permission
- blueprint status from excusing silent semantic drift

## S. Relationship To Player Experience And Civilization Scale

The formalization chain is necessary for believable machine building, industrialization, standards, institutions, and advanced civilizations.

Without it, the project would be forced into one of two bad shapes:

- local improvisation that never scales
- shallow crafting abstractions that erase knowledge, institutions, and reuse lineage

The formalization chain lets Dominium support:

- player creativity that becomes portable knowledge
- one-off designs that become reusable component families
- local workshop practices that become industrial methods
- repeated measurements that become accepted inspection standards
- social habits that become institutions, doctrines, or governance procedures
- civilizational propagation without turning history into disconnected content drops

This is also why `Λ-5` depends on the chain.
Player desires often target not only objects, but also:

- trusted methods
- repeatable production
- organizational adoption
- social legitimacy
- standard-compatible systems
- long-term maintenance and revision

## T. Failure And Ambiguity

Formalization may fail, stall, fragment, fork, remain contested, or remain local.

Examples include:

- discovery that cannot be reproduced
- specification that remains incomplete
- validation that fails or remains inconclusive
- competing standards
- institutions that disagree about adoption
- production methods that diffuse unevenly
- incompatible revision lines

Ambiguous or competing formalizations must be modeled explicitly.
They must not be handwaved into one winner by convenience.

When ambiguity remains, lawful states include:

- provisional
- contested
- locally accepted
- organization-specific
- under review
- superseded in one institution but still active in another

This preserves realism without forcing false uniformity.

## U. Anti-Patterns And Forbidden Shapes

The following are forbidden:

- treating recipes as the full formalization model
- allowing a blueprint to become a universal standard automatically
- silently skipping validation, lineage, or provenance
- conflating recognition with formal specification
- conflating documentation with constitutional standard
- allowing production or diffusion without explicit formalized structure where the domain requires one
- allowing revision to erase historical identity or compatibility posture silently
- allowing a UI, tool, or runtime convenience path to grant standard or institutional status by fiat
- collapsing institutional adoption into a mere metadata flag with no social or organizational consequences

## V. Stability And Evolution

Stability class: `stable`.

This artifact is stable enough for later `Λ`, `Σ`, `Φ`, and `Υ` prompts to cite directly.

Later prompts may:

- define domain-specific formalization chains
- define domain-specific validation classes
- define domain-specific adoption and diffusion mechanics
- define provenance, compatibility, versioning, naming, and release details
- define explicit actor workflows and governance around formalized artifacts

Later prompts may not:

- reduce formalization to crafting-only semantics
- collapse recognition, ascent, formalization, and substitution into one concept
- silently auto-promote standards into universal adoption
- silently erase lineage during revision or supersession
- override the chain-stage meanings frozen here without explicit versioned update

Any future change to the meaning of the formalization chain requires:

- explicit artifact update
- explicit versioning
- explicit downstream impact review
