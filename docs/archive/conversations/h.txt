## 3. Main Topics We Discussed

### Topic 1 — Deterministic simulation as the foundation

The entire conversation is anchored by the requirement that Dominium must run deterministically. This means the same initial state and same inputs must produce the same state across machines, compilers, operating systems, and eventually retro and modern platforms.

The chat discussed fixed tick phases, virtual lanes, merge order, command buffers, event queues, and deterministic serialization. The simulation must not depend on render FPS, wall-clock time, OS scheduler behaviour, GPU state, or floating-point quirks. This explains many later decisions: C89 core, integer/fixed-point math, no sim floats, no platform headers in simulation, and renderer as pure observer.

The conclusion was not just “be deterministic” as an aspiration. It became a set of implementation constraints: fixed tick pipeline, no nondeterministic iteration, no random core behaviour except seeded deterministic RNG where explicitly allowed, no dynamic hot-path allocation, and canonical serialization for hashing and replay.

What remains uncertain is the exact code-level API and component layouts. The chat produced specs and prompts, not final source code.

### Topic 2 — Generalising all systems into reusable primitives

The user asked how to avoid creating endless bespoke subsystems. The answer became the five-primitives model:

* Frames + Lattices
* Archetypes + Components
* Graphs + Fields
* Blueprints + Diffs
* Ticks + Messages + Jobs

This was one of the most important conceptual outputs. It provides the future assistant with a mental model for everything else. Railways are graph/corridor systems. Buildings are microgrid frames plus archetypes and fixtures. Terrain is base fields plus delta fields. Power, data, and fluids are graph/field systems. Construction is blueprints and jobs. Space travel is an orbital graph/domain system.

The conclusion was that most game systems should be implemented as data and components on top of these primitives, not as isolated custom engines.

### Topic 3 — Spatial hierarchy and world scale

The user defined a precise base-16 spatial ladder:

* Subnano = 1/65536 m
* Submicro = 1/4096 m
* Submini = 1/256 m
* Subtile = 1/16 m
* Tile = 1 m
* Subchunk = 16 m
* Chunk = 256 m
* Subregion = 4096 m
* Region = 65536 m
* Subcontinent = 1,048,576 m
* Continent = 16,777,216 m
* Supercontinent = 268,435,456 m
* Surface = 4,294,967,296 m

This became the backbone of the spatial model. The user also specified that the world is not a continuous dense grid across the whole surface. Instead, chunks/subchunks/regions are logically stored as microgrids. This is essential because a full-size planetary surface cannot be stored densely.

The default world is Earth in Sol in the Milky Way. The base game MVP is one surface, but the architecture should support galaxies, systems, planets, and future server/shard arrangements.

### Topic 4 — Surface domains versus space domains

The chat separated surface simulation from space simulation. A surface is a physical high-resolution domain with terrain, buildings, roads, rails, workers, utilities, and local movement. Space is a logical domain: orbit rails, transfer paths, docking nodes, Lagrange points, asteroid belts, gas clouds, electromagnetic fields, and local bubbles.

The decision to use on-rails orbital mechanics mattered because it avoids the complexity of full n-body simulation. Instead of integrating every orbital body continuously, the game can use deterministic graph-like orbital states and transitions. This fits the rest of the engine: graphs, fields, frames, and state machines.

Docking, landing, and takeoff were treated as transitions between domains. A rocket or drop pod does not exist simultaneously in full surface physics and full orbital simulation. It transitions through explicit states.

### Topic 5 — Building system and physical structures

The user wanted arbitrary structures but also deterministic integer math. The chat resolved this by making the simulation truth discrete: blocks/modules/cells/faces/edges. Blocks are structural or functional components. Walls, floors, roofs, and diagonal walls live on cell faces or edges. Devices and machines are placed as components/entities.

This supports split levels, mezzanines, slopes, ramps, cliffs, tunnels, underground structures, and orbital structures without needing arbitrary continuous geometry as the simulation core.

The user also wanted buildings rigid for now but future collapse/destruction support. The conclusion was to include future-proof structural fields but not implement collapse in the MVP.

### Topic 6 — Terrain, cut/fill, and microgrids

The user wanted cut/fill, underground structures, canals, trenches, lakes, rivers, and structures that obey logically defined unedited terrain. The answer was a three-layer terrain model:

1. Immutable base terrain.
2. Earthworks deltas and cavities.
3. Structural microgrids.

This means the terrain generated by the world remains knowable even after edits. Player modifications are stored separately as deltas or cavities. Buildings/tunnels/basements live in microgrids anchored to the surface, not by mutating terrain into building data.

This is important for future hydrology, geology, stability, collapse, pathing, and undo/history.

### Topic 7 — Vector splines, roads, rails, bridges, tunnels, and platforms

The user wanted arbitrary vector splines for roads, rails, bridges, tunnels, and long stations. The chat’s conclusion was that splines are authoring tools. They are not the runtime sim truth. The engine evaluates alignments, samples them into deterministic chainpoints, classifies segments as cut/fill/bridge/tunnel/platform, and bakes them into linear microgrids and graphs.

This lets the user draw smooth roads and rails, but the simulation deals with chainpoints, cells, nodes, edges, and known structures. Connections occur at nodes; if something connects in the middle of a segment, the system creates/splits a node.

This was critical for deterministic pathing, collision, signalling, and construction.

### Topic 8 — Fixtures, doors, portals, and sockets

A subtle but important user requirement was: do not code doors into every tunnel type, and do not make doors appear where not explicitly placed. The chat solved this with anchors/sockets and fixtures.

A corridor archetype can define where fixtures *can* attach. A door, light, signal, vent, utility cabinet, or platform screen door is a separate fixture entity. It appears only if manually placed, included in a blueprint, or generated by an explicit pattern. This prevents unwanted automatic behaviour and keeps types composable.

This also supports portals between microgrids: a tunnel and a building can connect via an explicit portal prefab that edits openings, connects path graphs, and places door fixtures.

### Topic 9 — Utility networks and data systems

The chat discussed electrical systems with DC, AC phases, voltages, frequencies, power factor, and traction integration. It also discussed data networks with fibre, copper, wireless, categories, protocols, bandwidth, latency, and telephony-like behaviour.

The architecture unified these under graph/field networks. Power is a graph with nodes, edges, generation, loads, voltages, phases, transformers, breakers, and deterministic balancing. Data is a graph with packets/signals moving at deterministic hop latency. Fluids use pressure/flow graph models. Thermal and atmosphere can be fields or graph-assisted fields.

For the MVP, these should be minimal but interactable: likely a generator, consumer, cable, simple fluid source/sink, and data node/link prototypes.

### Topic 10 — Transport systems

Railways began as a major topic but later became part of the larger transport framework. Roads, waterways, airways, and spaceways were all discussed. The final architecture treats transport as graph/corridor/path systems. Rail and road use alignments and nodes. Water can use depth fields plus navigation graphs. Air can use flight corridors, runways, and holding patterns. Space uses orbital graphs.

The MVP needs only minimal prototypes, not full transport realism. One straight segment with one deterministic vehicle may be enough to prove the system.

### Topic 11 — Renderer/platform split and backend roadmap

The user later clarified the platform/rendering plan. They want to first develop DX9 for Windows NT 2000 SP4 through Windows 11+, using native Win32 or SDL2, then later add DX11, GL1, GL2, Vulkan 1, and DX12. Windows 98 SE+ comes after, with DX9 or GL1 and native Win32 or SDL1. Modern Linux/macOS come later with POSIX or SDL2 and GL2 or Vulkan. macOS Classic, MS-DOS, Android, and others come later.

The chat produced a platform/renderer addendum: platform backends handle OS/window/input/time/file; renderer backends handle DirectX/OpenGL/Vulkan/software drawing; the engine generates draw commands and never calls platform/graphics APIs directly. MVP rendering is vector-only.

### Topic 12 — Repository, docs, and Codex preparation

The user provided an actual file tree and said Codex should work only from those files. The chat then updated the main docs and generated Codex prompts. This is a practical implementation layer: the architecture was converted into documents and prompts suitable for a code-generation agent.

The most important docs are `BUILDING.md`, `SPEC_CORE.md`, `DATA_FORMATS.md`, and `DIRECTORY_CONTEXT.md`, plus `docs/dev/dominium_new_addendum.txt`.

### Topic 13 — Lua data and deterministic script binding

The user wants Lua for all MVP data. When Codex asked where the binding should live and which Lua version to use, the assistant answered: add `/engine/script`, vendor Lua 5.4.4, expose a limited deterministic API, disable OS/native access, and hash full sim state via FNV-1a.

### Detailed decision explanations

#### Meter + fixed-point substeps

FACT: The user selected the meter-based option. This means one tile is one metre, with smaller subdivisions represented through fixed-point units. This made sense because the game is largely about buildings, infrastructure, vehicles, and terrain, where metre-scale cells are natural. Sub-metre detail remains possible without making the entire world millimetre-indexed.

This affects spatial constants, coordinates, serialization, renderer transforms, and physics. It could only be revisited if the whole spatial hierarchy were reopened.

#### Strict simulation/render/platform separation

FACT: The user selected strict separation. Rendering and platform APIs cannot influence simulation. This decision was made because the user wants deterministic simulation across a wide range of systems, including old platforms. Graphics APIs, OS windows, timing, input polling, and GPU state are all platform-specific and nondeterministic from the simulation’s perspective.

This affects the whole codebase. Platform code belongs in `/engine/platform`; graphics code belongs in `/engine/render`; simulation belongs in `/engine/core` and `/engine/sim`.

#### Base-16 spatial hierarchy

FACT: The user provided the full scale ladder. This was accepted as the spatial basis. Its base-16 structure aligns with fixed-point subdivisions and chunk hierarchy. It affects chunk sizes, region scales, world streaming, serialization, and coordinate conversion.

#### Sparse microgrid surface storage

FACT: The user said the whole surface should not be a continuous grid. Chunks/subchunks/regions are stored as logical microgrids. This is necessary because a full-size planetary surface cannot be held as a dense tile array. It also supports underground and overground structures.

#### Earth/Sol/Milky Way defaults

FACT: The user explicitly set Earth, Sol, and Milky Way as defaults for local single-player or multiplayer worlds. This gives the game a canonical baseline. It also matters because the user mentioned reserving Sol for official servers, meaning community servers need unique topology/identity to avoid collision.

#### Canonical surface plus instances/shards

FACT / INFERENCE: The user originally said one surface per planet but asked about sharding, multiple unique surfaces, and mirrored surfaces. The assistant proposed one canonical surface plus multiple surface instances. The user did not reject this and continued, so the package treats it as accepted design but with “final-ish” confidence.

This allows sharded servers and mirrored/test surfaces without abandoning the simple one-surface default.

#### On-rails space instead of n-body orbital mechanics

FACT: The chat decided on rails-based orbital mechanics for the main design. The rationale was that full n-body physics is too complex and nondeterministic for the game’s needs. On-rails orbits, transfer nodes, and local bubbles give predictable gameplay.

This affects future space implementation and prevents future assistants from trying to build a full orbital physics engine in the MVP.

#### Blocks/modules as building simulation truth

FACT: The building system settled on blocks/modules/cells/faces/edges. This was chosen because arbitrary vector structures are hard for deterministic collision, pathing, pressure, structure, and serialization. Blocks and face/edge walls give flexible buildings while preserving discrete simulation.

This affects buildings, stations, tunnels, ships, orbital structures, and future collapse.

#### Explicit fixtures

FACT: The user explicitly did not want doors coded into each tunnel type or appearing automatically. The decision was to use anchors/sockets and explicit fixture placement. This affects corridors, stations, buildings, devices, UI tools, and blueprints.

#### Splines baked into deterministic chainpoints

FACT: Splines exist for authoring roads/rails/tunnels/bridges, but runtime uses sampled chainpoints and microgrids. This makes arbitrary curves compatible with deterministic sim and efficient pathing.

#### MVP scope

FACT: The user explicitly stated the MVP. This is a major decision because it constrains implementation. Many big systems are deferred. MVP is vector-only and data-driven by Lua, with minimal prototypes for all systems.

#### `/engine/script`, Lua 5.4.4, sandbox

FACT: This was answered in response to Codex’s clarifying questions. It is a concrete implementation direction, though Lua 5.4.4 portability remains unverified.

#### FNV-1a full-state hash

FACT: The state hash decision was made to support early determinism tests. It should hash canonical serialized authoritative state, not arbitrary memory or renderer state.

## 6. Ideas Considered but Rejected, Superseded, or Deprioritised

The chat preserved several important “do not repeat this debate” outcomes.

Pure vector simulation was rejected as the authoritative building/world model. It was considered because the user wanted arbitrary shapes, but it was rejected for runtime truth because continuous vector geometry is too hard to make deterministic, efficient, serializable, and retro-compatible. Vectors remain useful for authoring, visual skins, and generating discrete structures.

Pure block-only design was also insufficient. The user wanted arbitrary splines and curved infrastructure. The final answer was hybrid: vector authoring, discrete runtime.

Full n-body orbital mechanics was rejected for the main space model. It could perhaps return as an optional specialized system, but it is not the core and not MVP.

Synchronous cross-surface simulation was rejected because it would undermine sharding and determinism. Cross-surface interactions should be asynchronous messages, trade, or scheduled transitions.

Automatic doors in tunnels were rejected very clearly. Doors and devices must be explicitly placed fixtures.

Runtime arbitrary spline math was rejected for simulation hot loops. Splines must bake into deterministic structures.

Textures, sounds, music, multiplayer, Sol-space, other renderers, and retro/modern non-Windows platforms were all deprioritised for MVP. They remain future work, not abandoned.

Collapse/destruction was also deprioritised. Buildings should be rigid now but should include enough data fields to support future structural failure.

A separate `CODEGEN_ADDENDUM.md` file was rejected because the user said Codex should use only listed files. The addendum should live in `docs/dev/dominium_new_addendum.txt`.

## 7. Important Reasoning and Rationale

Several visible tradeoffs shaped the final architecture.

The first tradeoff was **expressiveness versus determinism**. The user wanted arbitrary buildings, curves, tunnels, and infrastructure. Pure vector geometry maximizes expressiveness, but it creates difficult runtime problems. The solution was to keep expressive vector tools at authoring time while converting them into discrete data for simulation.

The second tradeoff was **realism versus implementation control**. Full n-body orbital mechanics, fluid dynamics, structural collapse, and fully realistic electrical systems could all become extremely complex. The chat repeatedly steered toward deterministic approximations that preserve gameplay meaning: rails-based orbits, graph-based networks, simple pressure/flow, rigid buildings for MVP, and discrete job systems.

The third tradeoff was **scope versus completeness**. The user wants all systems represented in MVP, but not as full systems. The phrase “all systems interactable, but minimal data prototypes” is important. It means the MVP should prove integration, not finish every domain.

The fourth tradeoff was **future-proofing versus first implementation**. The architecture keeps future support for Windows 98, Linux, macOS, retro systems, space, textures, audio, and multiplayer. But first implementation focuses on Windows/DX9/vector/core. This prevents architecture dead ends without requiring all future systems now.

## 16. Final Plain-English Summary

Dominium Architecture II was the chat where a sprawling game/engine design was consolidated into a coherent deterministic architecture and prepared for Codex implementation. The project being designed is Dominium: a deterministic, modular, multi-scale simulation engine and game intended to support everything from buildings, roads, railways, utilities, workers, and terrain modification to orbital stations, cargo tugs, drop pods, and future multiplayer universes.

The earliest visible parts of the chat continue from prior discussions about railway gauges, rail weights, traction, signalling, electrical systems, data networks, roadways, waterways, airways, and spaceways. The conversation then reached the central technical challenge: how to support arbitrary buildings, slopes, ramps, cliffs, cut/fill, tunnels, bridges, stations, and vector infrastructure while keeping the engine deterministic and integer-based. The answer became a hybrid model. The simulation truth is discrete: cells, blocks, faces, edges, components, graphs, fields, microgrids, chunks, and chainpoints. Vectors and splines exist for authoring, layout, generation, and visuals, but they bake into deterministic runtime structures.

The chat established a small set of unifying primitives: Frames + Lattices, Archetypes + Components, Graphs + Fields, Blueprints + Diffs, and Ticks + Messages + Jobs. These primitives unify buildings, railways, roads, tunnels, bridges, utilities, data networks, workers, factories, terrain, water, air, and future space systems. This prevents every domain from becoming a unique subsystem.

Several important decisions were made. The world uses metres as base units with fixed-point substeps. The spatial hierarchy runs from subnano through surface in powers of 16. Surfaces are not dense arrays but sparse logical microgrids/chunks. Earth, Sol, and the Milky Way are the default local world. One canonical surface per planet exists conceptually, but the architecture supports future multiple surface instances for sharding and mirroring. Cross-surface interactions are asynchronous only.

The building system became block/module/cell/face/edge based. Walls, floors, roofs, and diagonal walls attach to faces or edges. Doors, lights, signals, vents, utility panels, and other devices are explicit fixtures attached to anchors or sockets. They do not automatically appear in tunnels. Terrain is split into immutable base terrain, earthworks deltas/cavities, and structural microgrids. This supports cut/fill, tunnels, canals, trenches, underground structures, and future collapse without corrupting worldgen terrain.

Roads, rails, tunnels, bridges, and platforms are corridor systems. Users may draw vector splines, but the engine samples them into chainpoints and bakes them into linear microgrids and graph edges. This preserves arbitrary shapes while keeping simulation deterministic.

Space was separated from surface simulation. The future space model uses on-rails orbital mechanics and local bubbles rather than full n-body physics. Docking, landing, and takeoff are deterministic state-machine transitions. Orbital ships, stations, yards, rockets, drop pods, and cargo tugs are future systems built on the same ECS/job/logistics framework.

The immediate MVP was clearly defined by the user: a fully complete core, one full-size Earth surface, all systems interactable through minimal prototypes, Lua for all data, DX9.0c, SDL2, Win32, 2D vector top-down, and 3D vector first-person. Textures, sounds, music, multiplayer, the Sol system, other renderers, and other platforms are later.
